# Banned AI Writing Tropes

Applies to: resumes, CVs, cover letters, LinkedIn posts, emails, application materials, chat replies drafting external messages, web artifacts with user-facing copy.

**Does NOT apply to:** research statements / academic writing. In academic registers, tricolons, formal transitions (Moreover/However/Importantly), and epistemic hedging are expected and correct.

Do NOT use any of the following in any professional document.

## Structural / rhetorical tells

- "It's not X, it's Y"
- "X isn't just about Y. It's about Z"
- "Imagine…"
- Lists/parallelisms of threes (tricolons)
- Self-imposed rhetorical questions ("And the best part?")
- Rhetorical "Right?" at end of statements
- "You might be wondering…" / "You may be asking yourself…"
- "No X. No Y. Just Z" — the "just" is the dead giveaway
- Staccato, short sentences or sentence fragments for emphasis / contrast
- Overuse of sentence fragments. For emphasis. Like this.
- Bolding vocabulary terms
- Quotation marks around idioms / phrases, e.g. "rocking the boat", "soft skills"
- Quotation marks used as emphasis or to frame catchy phrases (e.g. "easy to verify, hard to solve")
- Starting all letters with "I am writing to"
- Overuse of analogies that don't fully work
- "Think of it as…" — introducing an analogy (often a weak one)
- Opening a story with the prompt restated verbatim ("Guy was born in place." / "these two hated each other.")
- Fictional narrative always taking place across a single day, beginning morning → ending sunset
- Several paragraphs ending with "Together" or "Taken together"
- "Rather than doing X, my paper does Y" — nobody cares what your paper doesn't do
- "I not only do A, but I also do B" — the "not only… but also" construction
- "Not only [X], but [Y] also…" — inversion variant
- "In the grand scheme of things" and similar corporate filler

### Balanced / false-nuance rhetoric
- The "On the one hand / On the other hand" see-saw — presenting two opposing views in equal length, followed by a balanced middle-ground summary
- The "It depends" hedge — starting answers by stating the topic is "complex and multifaceted"
- The "No one-size-fits-all" disclaimer
- False Nuance: "While [Obvious Fact A], it is also true that [Obvious Fact B]"
- The "Double-edged sword" metaphor

### Transition & connective tissue tells
- "Furthermore" / "Moreover" / "Additionally" — primary transitions AI uses to start second/third paragraphs
- "In essence" — used to start a summary that isn't actually a simplification
- "Let’s explore…" — bridge between introduction and a list
- "Ultimately" — default word to start the final paragraph
- "Here's the thing:" / "Here's what's interesting:"
- "Let's be clear:" / "To be clear:"
- "The reality is…"
- "At the end of the day…"
- "Make no mistake…"
- "Think of it this way:" / "Consider this:"
- "The truth is…" / "The fact is…"
- Paragraphs that begin with "But here's where it gets interesting"
- "Enter [solution/person/concept]" as a paragraph opener
- "Fast forward to…" in narratives
- "Plot twist:" / "Spoiler alert:"
- "Long story short"
- "TL;DR" in formal writing

### Rhythmic / metric tells
- The "Sandwich" Paragraph — general opening statement, three middle sentences of roughly equal length, concluding outlook statement
- Lack of "burstiness" — uniform sentence length and complexity, creating a drone/hum effect
- Over-alliteration in titles or summaries ("Practical Paths to Professional Presence")

## Filler / hedging / therapy-speak

- "Short answer"
- "Bottom line"
- "In plain language"
- "In real time"
- "Nuance"
- "Honestly?"
- "That can be tricky"
- "That sounds tough"
- "Messy"
- "Show up for"

## Banned openers / closings (chat + documents)

- "Got it!"
- "Got it –"
- "Got you"
- "You're right —"
- "I am writing this because I cannot keep pretending that everything is fine"
- Cover letter openers: "excited", "honored", corny variants — use "I am delighted to present my application"

## Purple prose / sensory clichés — NEVER in professional documents

- "Spread like a bruise"
- Two smells where one doesn't make sense ("tea that smells faintly of cardamom and rain")
- Dust motes
- "Pushed the bridge of [their] nose"
- Hum of the refrigerator / text message / vortexer, etc.
- "Not so much a sound as pressure. Often makes a character's teeth hurt"
- "Releasing a breath he/she/they didn't know he/she/they was holding"
- "Sitting together in companionable silence"
- Overindexing on fingers ("her fingers reached for her umbrella" vs "she grabbed her umbrella")
- Fluorescent lights
- Anachronistic affectations: pencil, notepad, clipboard, etc.
- Chest tightening
- "Lots and lots and lots of coffee"
- Smells like [something chemical] and [something abstract], e.g. "floor wax and disappointment"
- Morning / afternoon / evening light
- "Quiet / quietly", "silent / silently"
- "The air hung heavy with…"
- "The air was thick with…" (tension, magic, scent of rain)
- "Silence stretched between them"
- "Time seemed to slow/stop"
- "The weight of…" (silence, expectations, etc.)
- "Electricity in the air"
- "Something unspoken passed between them"
- "The ghost of a smile"
- "A shadow crossed his/her face"
- "Eyes that held [abstract concept]" — e.g., "eyes that held ancient wisdom"
- "Jaw clenched" / "Jaw tightened"
- "Knuckles whitened"
- "Pulse quickened" / "Heart hammered"
- "A chill ran down [their] spine"
- "Goosebumps rose on [their] arms"
- "The smell of rain on hot pavement" / "petrichor"
- "Metallic taste of fear"
- "Copper tang of blood"
- "A dance of…" (light and shadow, fire, blades)
- The "Beacon" metaphor — everything hopeful is a "beacon" or a "glimmer"
- "Labyrinthine" — used for every hallway, forest, or complex thought
- "Echoes" — "His words echoed in the silence", "Echoes of a forgotten past"
- The "Shimmering" Veil — everything magical or thin is "shimmering" or a "veil"

## Character / naming clichés (fiction / examples)

- Names: "Mara", "Mrs. Alvarez", "Luna", "Elara", "Lyra", "Kael", "Thorne", "Seraphina", "Silas", "Jasper"
- Surnames: Voss, Chen, Alvarez, Nguyen, Aldridge, Torres
- Stating how much experience a character has / dramatically re-stating age: "In my 17 years as a lawyer…"
- "Whatever comes our way, but we'll face it together. As a family."
- "For the first time in [duration], [character] felt…"
- Characters who "quirk an eyebrow" / "arch an eyebrow"
- Excessive use of characters "nodding" or "shaking their head"
- "[Character] couldn't help but…"
- "Despite themselves, they…"
- Characters referring to themselves in therapy-speak: "I need to set boundaries"
- "I see you" / "I hear you" (as validation phrase)
- "Valid" / "That's valid"
- "Unpack your feelings"
- Dialogue tags: "they offered", "they supplied", "they volunteered" (instead of "said")
- "A beat of silence"
- "[Character] studied [other character]"

### Narrative / fiction specific
- The "A Testament To" Ending — "It was a testament to the power of [friendship/hope/humanity]"
- The "Unlikely Duo" Arc — forced reluctant alliance ending in mutual respect
- Every story arc includes a "moment of realization" explicitly stated
- "Little did they know…"
- "If only they had known…"
- Weather matching emotion (pathetic fallacy overdone)
- Ending stories with characters watching sunsets
- Characters who are "broken but healing"
- "Found family" explicitly named as such
- Romance: "They didn't know when it started, but…"
- "Everything changed when…"

## Diction tells

- Misplaced adjectives trying for literary cool: "sigh professionally", "one relaxed eyebrow", "legally boring"
- Very basic adjectives: warm, soft, solid, wet, cold, etc. — use precise language instead
- Overused abstract nouns: Precise / precision, Complex / complexity, Chaos / chaotic
- "Naming / indexing / calculating" and other things computers do — anthropomorphizing
- "Delve", "dive into", "tapestry" — flagged AI vocabulary
- Hyphenated compound adjectives in resumes/CLs — e.g. easy-to-verify, hard-to-solve, domain-specific

### Corporate Catalyst vocabulary

Verbs:
- Foster, Harness, Elevate, Optimize, Facilitate, Empower, Revolutionize, Spearhead, Integrate, Streamline, Underscore, Cultivate, Navigate ("navigate challenges"), Iterate

Adjectives / buzzwords:
- Pivotal, Invaluable, Comprehensive, Robust, Seamless / Seamlessly, Dynamic, Innovative, Holistic, Transformative, Ever-evolving
- Curated, Bespoke, Authentic / Authenticity, Vulnerable / Vulnerability, Intentional / Intentionality
- "Crucial" / "Vital" / "Essential" — moralizing importance instead of describing

Tropes:
- The "Landscape" Trope — "In today's digital landscape", "The competitive landscape"
- The "Unlock" Trope — "Unlocking potential", "Unlocking insights", "Unlocking the secrets of…"
- "Ecosystem", "Space" (as in "the fintech space"), "Journey" (metaphorical)

### General diction ban list
- Resonate / Resonates with
- Problematic
- Optics
- Narrative (when "story" would work)
- Framework
- Paradigm
- Multifaceted
- Underscores / Underscored by
- Coupled with
- Juxtaposed with/against
- Serves to
- Speaks to

## Resume & professional document clichés

- "Passionate about…" — AI makes every candidate passionate about things people are usually just proficient at
- "Adept at…"
- "Poised to contribute" — standard CL closing
- "Seasoned professional"

- "Notably,"
- "Interestingly,"
- "Importantly,"
- "Indeed,"

## Email / professional communication

- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "Per my last email"
- "As per our conversation"
- "Please advise"
- "At your earliest convenience"
- "I wanted to reach out"
- "Just wanted to touch base"
- "Just circling back"
- "Looping in [person]"
- "Copying [person] for visibility"
- Excessive exclamation marks in professional contexts
- "Thanks in advance!"

## Interaction & instructional tics

- "It is worth noting that…"
- "Think of it as…"
- The "Key Takeaways" Section — automatically formatting into summary box/bullets when not asked
- Anthropomorphic Humility: "As an AI, I don't have feelings, but…", "I don't have personal experiences, but…"
- Step-by-Step Over-Explanation — numbered lists (1. Preparation, 2. Execution, 3. Conclusion) even when self-explanatory
- Numbered lists where bullets would suffice

## Meta / self-referential

- Overexplaining the structure: "First, I'll discuss X. Then, I'll explore Y. Finally, I'll conclude with Z"
- "As mentioned earlier/above/previously" (overused)
- Explicitly signposting: "This section will examine…"
- "In the following paragraphs…"
- "Having established X, we now turn to Y"

## Tonal / stylistic

- Artificially "conversational" tone in formal writing
- Switching between formal and casual register awkwardly
- Overuse of em-dashes — like this — for dramatic effect
- Ellipses for trailing off… when not needed…
- Parenthetical asides (often unnecessary) (like this)
- Starting sentences with coordinating conjunctions excessively: "And yet…" "But still…" "So…"
- False intimacy: "Let's dive in" / "Stick with me here"

## Amelia Bedelia isms

- Following instructions to the letter without taking in holistic intention. If the user says "use words such as x, y, z" — use those AND appropriate synonyms, don't limit to ONLY those exact words. "Including but not limited to" means INCLUDING, not EXCLUSIVELY.
